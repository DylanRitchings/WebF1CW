Literature Review

Mitigating Corporate Information Exposure

The purpose of this report is to show how corporations aim to mitigate the impacts of or prevent information exposure. I will be focusing on companies that outsource their data to other firms, use cloud computing and companies that have data that different people within the company have access to. I will be writing about encryption, different strategies to stop information from being viewable by unauthorised people with hardware and software and how information outsourcing firms should be transparent with their clients when they data gets leaked.

Information exposure usually begins with social engineering where people use influence and persuasion to deceive people to gain information (Kevin D. Mitnick, 2001). Mitnick writes about how employees are as important as software and hardware. Using security technologies and security policies for employee behaviour can mitigate information exposure. They should be trained to be aware that there are people trying to manipulate them into giving away information. They should know how to protect their information and be aware what the companyâ€™s security policies and procedures are. Employees should also know that they are protecting their own private information as well as their job.

In Economics of Privacy (Yosra Miaoui, 2015) it is said that outsourcing information to different companies comes with a level of risk and can decrease the privacy of the data so companies that do not outsource their private data could have less privacy issues. It also says that once private data is stolen how the data is misused and when it is misused depends on the lifetime of the data therefor to figure out how to protect the data or if it should be outsourced at all companies must assess the degree of vulnerability and how much it can be misused by a cyber attacker. They also write companies who use outsourced data must tell the company whose data they have anything that has happened with the data straight away. This means the company will be able to act on any data exposure and can start to make the data stolen useless. This mostly will reduce the financial damage that has occurred but more damage will occur until the end of the data lifetime.

Most corporations including health organisations will encrypt any information being outsourced many of them use attribute based encryption. (Ming Li Member, 2012) Attribute based encryption allows multiple users to access the encrypted data this is unlike other methods used to encrypt health records before.

Many corporations use cloud computing to store their data. There are many security issues with cloud computing including there is no knowledge where the data runs, who controls the encryption and decryption keys and many more. (Rachna Arora, 2013) RSA Algorithm distributes a public key to all workstations that can encrypt the message and a private key which is not shared. When the user wants to access the file the cloud provider will check if the user has access and if so will send the data.

In large corporations, different information should be put into separate categories so people in different departments can only view their data (Kevin D. Mitnick, 2001). In government organisations, this can be either classified or unclassified. Organizations put information in levels of sensitivity this stops people from accessing data they should not be allowed to see (Jerome H. Saltzer, 2017). Data can be written to a lower level of sensitivity by accident or if a user is infected by a trojan horse. To stop data from getting to lower levels the Bell and LaPadula model is used (Irvine, 2014), a person reading highly sensitive information sets what level they are in so they are unable to write to lower sensitivity levels. Also, events can be used to alert the user if a security violation is occurring and any printed documents should have a blank face with a title of the level of sensitivity it is from. There are two types of threats that occur with multilevel security systems. Developmental are flaws in the system through mistakes in design and implementation this can be avoided by testing the system before it is put in use. Operational are flaws where insiders are used to let external activities occur.

Stopping information exposure from occurring can be simple and have nothing to do with the software side of computers, doors containing computers can be locked, controlling who can change the hardware can stop people without authorization from taking information from hard drives, shield the computers so electromagnetic radiation cannot be used to gain access, telling staff to keep IDs hidden when outside the building so the ID cannot be reproduced. (Jerome H. Saltzer, 2017).

Furthermore, organisations can aim to deter people from trying to gain their information (Committee on Deterring Cyberattacks: Informing Strategies and Developing, 2010). This can be achieved by threatening the attacker, this is usually only done by governments. This harm can be achieved by making the attack too costly by defending, or by retaliating. Deterrence by retaliating can cost less than defending but might not get as much deterrence for other attacks from this.

In conclusion, organisations should know that threats of information exposure cannot be completely removed. The threats can be reduced by teaching employees to be aware of the threats and how to avoid being socially engineered, using correct software in an efficient way to stop cyber-attacks or accidental exposure, encrypting information, putting different types of information into different categories, locking doors to stop people from accessing systems they should not have access to, deterring people from trying to gain the information and more that have not been discussed in this review.
